# Meditation Practices: The Still Point Within

## The Nature of Meditation

Meditation is not the suppression of thought. This is perhaps the most widespread misunderstanding about the practice, and it is worth addressing directly before exploring specific techniques.

Meditation is the cultivation of a particular quality of awareness — one that can observe experience (including thought, sensation, and emotion) without being caught in it. The mind continues to think. The senses continue to receive. But the relationship to all of this changes: there is a growing capacity to be present to what is, rather than perpetually captured by the content of the mind.

This quality of awareness — stable, open, non-reactive — has been called by many names across traditions: *sati* (mindfulness) in Buddhism, *sakshi* (witness consciousness) in Vedanta, *contemplatio* in the Christian mystical tradition, *shikan taza* (just sitting) in Zen. The underlying phenomenology is consistent: a quality of presence that is awake, clear, and spacious.

Modern neuroscience is beginning to map the neural correlates of this state. Meditation is associated with changes in the default mode network (the brain network active during self-referential thought and mind-wandering), increased grey matter density in the prefrontal cortex and insula, reduced amygdala reactivity, and measurable changes in gamma wave synchrony during deep practice. These changes are not merely functional — they are structural, and they persist beyond the meditation session.

## Body Scan

### Origins
The body scan is most closely associated with Jon Kabat-Zinn's Mindfulness-Based Stress Reduction (MBSR) programme, developed at the University of Massachusetts Medical School in 1979. Kabat-Zinn drew from Vipassana Buddhist tradition, particularly the Burmese school of S.N. Goenka and U Ba Khin, which emphasises systematic body scanning as the foundation of insight meditation.

But systematic attention to body sensations appears across traditions: in Tibetan Buddhism as the *nyamgyu* (path of feeling), in Yoga as the cultivation of *pratyahara* (sensory withdrawal), and in Taoist practice as the inner observation of qi movement.

### Method
1. Lie down or sit, eyes closed. Allow the body to settle and the breath to become natural.
2. Begin at the top of the head. Notice any sensations present without trying to change them — tingling, pressure, warmth, neutral absence of sensation. All are equally valid.
3. Slowly move attention down: forehead, eyes, cheeks, jaw, neck, shoulders. At each area, simply notice what is there.
4. Continue: upper arms, forearms, hands, fingers. Notice the sensations in each finger individually if possible.
5. Upper chest, middle chest, lower chest. Notice the movement of breath.
6. Upper back, middle back, lower back.
7. Abdomen — feel the rise and fall with each breath.
8. Hips, gluteal region, upper thighs, knees, calves, ankles, feet, each toe.
9. After reaching the feet, either return to the head and repeat, or rest in whole-body awareness.

**Duration**: 20-45 minutes for a full body scan. A shorter 10-minute version is effective for daily practice.

**Science**: MBSR with body scan has been shown in hundreds of studies to reduce chronic pain, anxiety, depression, and improve immune function, sleep quality, and quality of life. Changes in insula and somatosensory cortex activity are measurable after 8 weeks of regular practice.

## Mindfulness of Breath (Anapanasati)

### Origins
Anapanasati — literally "mindfulness of breathing" — is one of the oldest and most fundamental meditation practices in the Buddhist tradition. The *Anapanasati Sutta* (Mindfulness of Breathing Discourse, from the Pali Canon) is attributed directly to the Buddha, who taught this practice to 500 monks. It is perhaps the most completely documented meditation instruction in any tradition.

The sixteen steps of Anapanasati outlined in the Sutta represent a complete path from simple breath awareness through full liberation — arguably the most compact instruction for enlightenment ever committed to text.

### Method (Simplified)
1. Sit in a stable, comfortable position. Close the eyes or soften the gaze downward.
2. Take a few intentional breaths to establish connection with the body.
3. Release control of the breath and allow it to breathe naturally — do not regulate it.
4. Choose an anchor point: the sensation of air at the nostrils, the rise and fall of the chest, or the movement of the belly. This is your primary object.
5. Rest attention on this anchor. When the mind wanders (and it will — this is what minds do), gently and without criticism return attention to the anchor.
6. Each return is not a failure — it is the practice. The act of noticing the mind has wandered and returning it is the meditation.
7. Continue for the duration of the session.

**Duration**: Begin with 10 minutes. Build toward 20-30 minutes daily. Longer sessions (45-60+ minutes) produce qualitatively different states after a foundation is established.

**Science**: Mindfulness meditation produces measurable reductions in amygdala volume and reactivity, increases in prefrontal cortex thickness, and changes in the default mode network associated with reduced rumination. After 8 weeks of MBSR (which is primarily mindfulness of breath), MRI studies show structural changes in the brain.

## Loving Kindness (Metta)

### Origins
Metta (Pali: loving kindness, benevolence) meditation comes from the Theravada Buddhist tradition and is taught across Buddhist schools. The *Metta Sutta* — the discourse on loving kindness — begins:

*"May all beings be happy. May all beings be well. May all beings be safe. May all beings be at ease."*

The practice is far older than its Buddhist formulation. The cultivation of unconditional goodwill toward all beings appears in the Jain concept of *ahimsa* (non-harming), in the Hindu *mudita* (sympathetic joy), in the Christian *agape* (universal love), and in the Confucian *ren* (benevolence). The specific meditation technique is Buddhism's contribution — the systematic cultivation of this quality through deliberate practice.

### Method
1. Sit comfortably, close the eyes. Take a few breaths to settle.
2. **Phase 1 — Self**: Begin with yourself. Call to mind the wish for your own wellbeing. You may use traditional phrases internally, or find your own:
   - *May I be happy. May I be healthy. May I be safe. May I live with ease.*
   Feel the phrases as intentions, not performances.
3. **Phase 2 — Benefactor**: Call to mind someone who has been kind or generous to you — a teacher, a mentor, a grandparent. Visualise them and extend the same wishes:
   - *May you be happy. May you be healthy. May you be safe. May you live with ease.*
4. **Phase 3 — Neutral person**: Someone you neither like nor dislike — someone you see regularly but don't know. Extend the same wishes.
5. **Phase 4 — Difficult person**: Someone with whom you have difficulty or conflict. This is the stretch. Extend the wishes — not because they deserve them, but because the loving quality in you deserves to be unconstrained.
6. **Phase 5 — All beings**: Expand outward — your neighbourhood, your city, your country, all living beings on Earth. *May all beings be happy. May all beings be free from suffering.*

**Duration**: 20-30 minutes allows for full progression. A shorter version (10 minutes) focusing on self and benefactor is effective for daily practice.

**Science**: Research led by Barbara Fredrickson at the University of North Carolina has shown that loving kindness meditation measurably increases positive emotions, social connectedness, feelings of purpose, and physical health markers. It increases vagal tone (HRV), reduces implicit bias, and accelerates recovery from negative emotional events. Richard Davidson's research at the University of Wisconsin documented that experienced Tibetan monks practicing compassion meditation showed unprecedented levels of gamma wave synchrony — far beyond anything previously observed in normal subjects.

## NSDR and Yoga Nidra: The Non-Sleep Deep Rest Spectrum

### Origins
*Yoga Nidra* (Sanskrit: yogic sleep) is an ancient practice documented in the Mandukya Upanishad, the Agama texts of Shaivism, and the Tantras. It is the systematic cultivation of the hypnagogic state — the liminal awareness between waking and sleep — as a vehicle for deep rest, healing, and ultimately, the recognition of pure consciousness.

The traditional practice guides the practitioner through increasingly subtle layers of the self (the *pancha kosha* — five sheaths of existence) while remaining aware. The practitioner is not sleeping — they are aware of being in a sleep-like state.

Richard Miller, a clinical psychologist who studied with T.K.V. Desikachar (Krishnamacharya's son), adapted the traditional practice into **iRest** (Integrative Restoration) for clinical use. It has been used with US military veterans with PTSD and is now an approved practice in VA hospitals.

Andrew Huberman at Stanford has popularised a simplified version under the term **NSDR (Non-Sleep Deep Rest)** — deliberately non-spiritual terminology for a scientific audience. NSDR includes Yoga Nidra and related practices that produce the theta brain state associated with the hypnagogic boundary.

### The Science of Hypnagogia
The hypnagogic state — the transition zone between waking and sleep — is characterised by:
- **Theta wave dominance** (4-8 Hz): Associated with deep relaxation, creative insight, and memory consolidation
- **Reduced default mode activity**: The self-referential mind quiets
- **Neuroplasticity enhancement**: The brain appears to be particularly receptive to learning and change in this state
- **Dopamine restoration**: Research by Huberman's group has shown that a 20-minute NSDR session restores dopamine levels in the basal ganglia — the brain region associated with motivation and drive — after sleep deprivation

### Method (Short Form)
1. Lie down in a comfortable position. You will not be moving. Use an eye mask if helpful.
2. Close the eyes. Take 3-5 slow deep breaths, then release control of the breath.
3. Begin rotating awareness through body parts in rapid succession — a faster scan than the body scan: right thumb, index finger, middle finger, ring finger, little finger, palm, back of hand, wrist, forearm, elbow, upper arm, shoulder, armpit... (continuing through the entire body in a prescribed order)
4. This rapid rotation prevents the mind from settling into a thought while also maintaining a thread of awareness.
5. Visualise contrasting sensations: heaviness and lightness; warmth and coolness; pain and pleasure.
6. Visualise rapid successions of images if prompted (traditional Nidra uses specific sequences).
7. Rest in open awareness — simply being.

**Duration**: 20-45 minutes. Huberman recommends 20 minutes as a reliable restorative dose.

**Use cases**: Recovery after intense work or exercise, jetlag and disrupted sleep, mid-afternoon energy restoration, any situation requiring rapid mental recovery without full sleep.

## Visualisation Practices

### Origins and Context
Visualisation as a deliberate practice appears in Tibetan Buddhist *deity yoga*, in Jungian active imagination, in Greek and Roman memory palace techniques, in shamanic journey work across many cultures, and in modern sports psychology (mental rehearsal).

The common thread: the mind cannot fully distinguish between a vividly imagined experience and an actual one. Research on mirror neurons, the effects of mental rehearsal on athletic performance, and the documented physiological responses to vivid imagery (heart rate, skin conductance, hormone levels) all support this.

### Method: Healing Visualisation
1. Settle into a comfortable position. Close the eyes. Take several slow breaths.
2. Bring to mind a specific intention — a quality you wish to cultivate, a healing you wish to support, a performance you wish to prepare for.
3. Construct a vivid inner image: see yourself in the desired state. Engage all senses — what do you see, hear, feel, smell, sense in this image?
4. Rest in this image for 10-20 minutes, returning when the mind wanders.
5. Bring the session to a close with a moment of gratitude or intention-setting.

## Trataka: Candle Gazing

### Origins
Trataka (Sanskrit: to look, to gaze) is one of the six classical Hatha Yoga cleansing practices (*shatkarma*). It involves steady, unblinking gazing at a fixed point — most traditionally a candle flame.

The practice is used across traditions: Tibetan *thangka* gazing, Sufi gaze meditation, Taoist *neigong* visualisation practices, and shamanic practices involving fire gazing all share the same essential structure.

### Method
1. Place a candle at eye level, approximately 2-3 feet away, in a darkened room without drafts.
2. Sit in a stable, comfortable position.
3. Gaze at the flame without blinking. The eyes will water — allow this. Do not rub the eyes.
4. When you cannot avoid blinking, close the eyes and visualise the afterimage of the flame at the point between the eyebrows.
5. When the image fades, open the eyes and resume gazing.
6. Continue for 5-20 minutes.

**Science**: Sustained visual focus activates the oculomotor system and the superior colliculus, while simultaneously reducing the scanning and darting movement associated with mind-wandering. The single-pointed attention required for trataka is a form of *dharana* (concentration) — the preparatory step to meditation in Patanjali's system.

## Open Awareness Meditation

### Origins
Open awareness meditation — known as *Rigpa* in Tibetan Buddhism, *Shikantaza* in Zen, *Sahaj Samadhi* in Vedanta — represents the non-dual perspective: rather than focusing the attention on a single object, the practitioner rests in awareness itself, without an object.

This is considered an advanced practice in most traditions — approached after concentration (dharana) and single-pointed meditation (dhyana) are established. But some teachers, particularly in the Tibetan Dzogchen tradition, teach it as a beginning practice — pointing directly to the nature of awareness rather than building toward it gradually.

### Method
1. Sit comfortably. Close the eyes. Settle for a few moments.
2. Rather than placing attention *on* anything, release the impulse to focus and simply allow awareness to be present without an object.
3. Thoughts arise — let them pass without following them. Sounds arise — let them pass without engaging. Sensations arise — let them be present without analysis.
4. You are not trying to achieve anything. You are simply resting as awareness — being the space in which experience arises, without being captivated by any particular experience.
5. If the mind grabs at something, note "grabbing" and return to openness.

**Duration**: Begin with 5-10 minutes. This practice is deceptively demanding — the impulse to focus on something is strong. Longer sessions become available as the capacity for open resting deepens.

## Building a Meditation Foundation

The wisest approach to meditation is to begin simply and consistently, rather than ambitiously and sporadically. Ten minutes of daily practice produces more lasting change than occasional hour-long sessions.

A simple foundation: 10 minutes of mindfulness of breath daily, in the same place, at the same time. After 30 days, extend to 15 minutes and add one other technique that appeals. After 90 days, the practice will have established itself as a natural part of the day — not something requiring willpower, but something missed when absent.

The purpose of these practices is not the technique itself but what the technique reveals: the quality of awareness that is always already present, prior to whatever we are thinking, feeling, or experiencing. Every tradition points to this. Every practice is an invitation to look more closely at what is already here.
