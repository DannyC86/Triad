# The Breathly Library: Definitive Reading and Learning

## Essential Books

### Foundational Texts

**Breath: The New Science of a Lost Art** — James Nestor (2020)
The single best entry point for a modern reader. Nestor spent years investigating breath science, submitted himself to experiments, travelled to caves where ancient skulls demonstrate superior nasal passages, and synthesised everything into a gripping narrative. Covers: nasal breathing, CO2 tolerance, mouth taping, the Buteyko method, chanting, the resonance frequency, historical and anthropological breath science. Essential reading.

**The Oxygen Advantage** — Patrick McKeown (2015)
The most comprehensive and scientifically grounded treatment of Buteyko principles applied to athletic performance and general health. McKeown covers the BOLT score, nasal breathing, altitude training principles, and breath hold training. Rigorous without being dry. Essential for anyone serious about the science.

**Autobiography of a Yogi** — Paramahansa Yogananda (1946)
One of the most influential books of the 20th century. Yogananda's account of his life and training under Sri Yukteswar introduces the Western reader to the full breadth of yogic science — pranayama, meditation, the subtle body, consciousness states, and the connection between breath and liberation. Not a technique manual — a vision of what breath science can become.

**The Biology of Belief** — Bruce Lipton (2005)
Cell biologist Lipton's account of how the environment — including the internal biochemical environment created by mind and breath — regulates gene expression. Essential for understanding the cellular science behind why breathwork produces lasting physiological change. Lipton's background as a Stanford professor gives his unconventional ideas substantial credibility.

**Why Zebras Don't Get Ulcers** — Robert Sapolsky (1994, updated 2004)
Stanford biologist Sapolsky's masterwork on the stress response. Not specifically about breath, but essential for understanding what chronic stress does to the body and brain — and therefore why breathwork matters so profoundly. Funny, devastating, and deeply informative.

**Stealing Fire** — Steven Kotler and Jamie Wheal (2017)
An investigation of how Navy SEALs, Silicon Valley executives, athletes, and meditators use altered states of consciousness for performance enhancement. Breath features significantly. Broad, energising, and well-researched.

**The Miracle of Mindfulness** — Thich Nhat Hanh (1975)
The Vietnamese Zen master's gentle, profound introduction to mindfulness of breath. Written as a letter to a colleague, it remains perhaps the most accessible and moving introduction to meditation practice in print. Short, timeless, essential.

**Science of Breath** — Swami Rama, Rudolph Ballentine, Alan Hymes (1979)
The rigorous yogic-scientific account of pranayama from the Himalayan Institute. Swami Rama — who was studied at the Menninger Foundation demonstrating voluntary control of normally involuntary physiological functions — brings both practitioner and scientific credibility. Covers pranayama techniques, anatomy, and the theory of prana with unusual precision.

### Deeper Reading

**The Healing Power of the Breath** — Richard Brown and Patricia Gerbarg (2012)
Two psychiatrists present the clinical research on coherent breathing, Sudarshan Kriya, and yoga breathing for anxiety, depression, PTSD, and addiction. Perhaps the most rigorous clinical text on breathwork available.

**Just Breathe** — Dan Brulé (2017)
A comprehensive introduction to conscious breathing from a leading breathwork teacher. Less scientific than McKeown or Nestor but more experiential — excellent for practitioners.

**The Wim Hof Method** — Wim Hof (2020)
Hof's own account of his method, his life, and his vision. Personal, inspiring, and practical. Includes the full protocol.

**Breath as Prayer** — Jennifer Tucker (2022)
The Christian contemplative tradition of breath prayer — how Hesychast breathing practices, lectio divina, and the Jesus Prayer connect to the broader breathwork conversation.

**Light on Pranayama** — B.K.S. Iyengar (1981)
The definitive yogic text on pranayama from one of the 20th century's most influential yoga masters. Comprehensive, rigorous, and demanding. Not for beginners — but essential for serious practitioners.

**The Breathing Book** — Donna Farhi (1996)
An excellent practical guide to understanding your breathing patterns and correcting habitual dysfunction. Accessible and embodied.

## People of Interest

### Wim Hof (The Iceman)
Born 1959, Netherlands. Holder of 26 Guinness World Records related to cold exposure. Developer of the Wim Hof Method — a simplified form of Tibetan Tummo combined with cold exposure and mindset training. Subjects of multiple peer-reviewed studies including the landmark Radboud University immune modulation research. Lost his wife Olaya to suicide in 1995; has spoken about how his practices helped him survive grief. Not a scientist — but a genuine phenomenon whose existence forced science to revise its understanding of voluntary physiological control. A thundering, generous, sometimes bombastic presence who is, underneath everything, utterly sincere.

### Andrew Huberman
Professor of neurobiology and ophthalmology at Stanford University School of Medicine. Host of the *Huberman Lab* podcast, which has become one of the most listened-to science communication platforms in the world. Huberman specialises in translating neuroscience research into practical protocols. His contributions to breathwork specifically include popularising the physiological sigh, explaining the neurological basis of cyclic sighing, and conducting research comparing different breathing protocols for stress and mood. Consistently cites original research, maintains scientific credibility while being accessible.

### Patrick McKeown
Irish breathing instructor and author, based in Galway. The most influential living teacher of the Buteyko Method. Overcame severe asthma, allergies, and sleep disorders through Buteyko training and has since dedicated his career to developing and spreading the method globally. His BOLT (Body Oxygen Level Test) is a refinement of Buteyko's Control Pause. His books — particularly *The Oxygen Advantage* — are the most scientifically rigorous accessible texts on Buteyko principles. Trained with Buteyko practitioners in Russia and continues to develop the method's application in athletic performance and sleep medicine.

### Paramahansa Yogananda (1893-1952)
Born in Gorakhpur, India. Trained under Sri Yukteswar, a master in the Kriya Yoga lineage. Arrived in Boston in 1920, the first Indian yoga master to take up permanent residence in the West. Founded the Self-Realization Fellowship in Los Angeles in 1920, which continues his work. His *Autobiography of a Yogi* has been in continuous print since 1946 and remains one of the most read spiritual books in history. Steve Jobs requested that a copy be the only book on his iPad and arranged for it to be distributed at his memorial. Yogananda's contribution: the first serious attempt to bridge the yogic inner science with a Western scientific audience.

### Stanislav Grof
Czech psychiatrist, born 1931. One of the founders of transpersonal psychology. Developed Holotropic Breathwork in the 1970s as a legal alternative to LSD after psychedelic research was made illegal — a technique using accelerated breathing and evocative music to produce non-ordinary states of consciousness. His research documented healing of trauma, encounters with perinatal experiences, and what he called "transpersonal" states — experiences that appear to transcend the individual mind-body system. His work remains controversial in mainstream psychology but has been influential in psychedelic and breathwork research communities.

### Dr. Ela Manga
South African physician and breathwork pioneer, specialising in the application of breathwork to burnout, occupational health, and integrative medicine in corporate and healthcare settings. Author of *Breathe* (2019). Has developed training programmes for healthcare workers using breathwork for stress resilience. A significant bridge between breathwork traditions and occupational health.

### Jack Kornfield
American psychologist and Buddhist teacher trained in the Theravada tradition in Thailand, Burma, and India under teachers including Ajahn Chah and Mahasi Sayadaw. Co-founder of the Insight Meditation Society (IMS) in Barre, Massachusetts, and Spirit Rock Meditation Center in California. Has spent five decades making Buddhist meditation accessible to Western students while maintaining psychological sophistication. His books — particularly *A Path with Heart* — are among the most widely read introductions to Buddhist practice. A warm, deeply skilled, and psychologically aware teacher.

### Dr. Stephen Porges
Distinguished University Scientist at the Kinsey Institute, Indiana University, and Research Professor of Psychiatry at the University of North Carolina at Chapel Hill. Developer of Polyvagal Theory — the framework for understanding the hierarchy of nervous system states (safety, mobilisation, shutdown) and their relationship to trauma, social engagement, and health. His work has revolutionised the understanding of trauma and the autonomic nervous system, and has been widely adopted in trauma therapy. The direct clinical implications of polyvagal theory for breathwork — particularly the vagal activation produced by slow, rhythmic breathing — make his work foundational.

### HeartMath Institute
Founded in 1991 in Boulder Creek, California, by Doc Childre. A nonprofit research and education organisation that has spent three decades studying the physiology and psychology of heart-brain coherence. Their research on HRV, cardiac coherence, and the electromagnetic field of the heart has been published in peer-reviewed journals. Their practical tools — the HeartMath techniques, the emWave biofeedback device — translate their research into accessible daily practices. Their finding that the heart generates its own nervous system (containing approximately 40,000 neurons) and that this heart-brain communicates bidirectionally with the cranial brain has significant implications for breathwork.

### James Nestor
American journalist and author. Not a scientist or practitioner but an extraordinarily gifted synthesist and communicator. His book *Breath* (2020) is likely the single most important work in making breath science accessible to a mainstream audience. His willingness to self-experiment — including the Stanford nose-plugging study — gives his writing unusual authenticity.

## Research Papers and Studies

### Polyvagal Theory
- Porges, S.W. (1995). "Orienting in a defensive world: Mammalian modifications of our evolutionary heritage." *Psychophysiology*, 32(4), 301-318. — The original paper presenting Polyvagal Theory.
- Porges, S.W. (2007). "The polyvagal perspective." *Biological Psychology*, 74(2), 116-143. — Comprehensive update.

### HeartMath Coherence Studies
- McCraty, R., et al. (1995). "The effects of emotions on short-term power spectrum analysis of heart rate variability." *American Journal of Cardiology*, 76(14), 1089-1093.
- McCraty, R., & Shaffer, F. (2015). "Heart rate variability: New perspectives on physiological mechanisms, assessment of self-regulatory capacity, and health risk." *Global Advances in Health and Medicine*, 4(1), 46-61.

### Radboud University Wim Hof Studies
- Kox, M., et al. (2014). "Voluntary activation of the sympathetic nervous system and attenuation of the innate immune response in humans." *PNAS*, 111(20), 7379-7384. — The landmark study.
- Muzik, O., et al. (2018). "Brain over body: A study on the willful regulation of autonomic function during cold exposure." *NeuroImage*, 172, 632-641.

### Huberman Lab / Cyclic Sighing
- Balban, M.Y., et al. (2023). "Brief structured respiration practices enhance mood and reduce physiological arousal." *Cell Reports Medicine*, 4(1). — Comparing cyclic sighing to other protocols.

### Buteyko Clinical Research
- Bowler, S.D., et al. (1998). "Buteyko breathing techniques in asthma: a blinded randomised controlled trial." *Medical Journal of Australia*, 169(11-12), 575-578.
- McHugh, P., et al. (2003). "Buteyko Breathing Technique for asthma: An effective intervention." *New Zealand Medical Journal*, 116(1187).

### Nadi Shodhana
- Telles, S., et al. (2013). "Effect of yoga-based interventions on cortisol rhythmicity." *Journal of Evidence-Based Complementary & Alternative Medicine*.
- Multiple studies from the National Institute of Mental Health and Neurosciences (NIMHANS), Bangalore, on HRV and EEG effects of alternate nostril breathing.

### Nitric Oxide and Humming
- Weitzberg, E., & Lundberg, J.O. (2002). "Humming greatly increases nasal nitric oxide." *American Journal of Respiratory and Critical Care Medicine*, 166(2), 144-145.

### Yoga Nidra / NSDR
- Moszeik, E.N., et al. (2020). "Effectiveness of a short Yoga Nidra meditation on stress, sleep, and well-being in a large and diverse sample." *Current Psychology*.
- Ferreira-Vorkapic, C., et al. (2018). "The impact of yoga nidra and seated meditation on the mental health of college professors." *International Journal of Yoga*.

## Podcasts and Audio Resources

**Huberman Lab** (Andrew Huberman) — Episode 9: Control Your Breathing, Episode 37: Using Deliberate Cold Exposure, Episode 57: NSDR, Yoga Nidra, Sleep. Huberman's episodes are long (2-3 hours) but exhaustively referenced.

**Wim Hof Podcast appearances** — Particularly Joe Rogan Experience #712 and #865, and Tim Ferriss Show. Hof is most himself in conversation.

**Tara Brach Podcast** — Buddhist meditation teacher, psychologist. Free guided meditations and dharma talks. Particularly valuable for the integration of psychological and contemplative perspectives.

**Jack Kornfield's Dharma Talks** (available via Spirit Rock) — Among the most experienced Western dharma teachers. Warm, psychologically sophisticated, practically grounded.

**James Nestor interviews** — Search "James Nestor Breath" for numerous podcast appearances where he summarises his research findings accessibly.

**The Mind Science Podcast** (Rupert Spira) — Non-dual philosopher and meditator. For those interested in the philosophical dimensions of consciousness and awareness.

**"The Science of Breathing"** — Andrew Huberman, Huberman Lab YouTube. Free, comprehensive, scientifically grounded overview.
