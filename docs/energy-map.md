# Energy Map: When to Use What

## The Two-Axis Framework

Every breathwork and meditation technique can be understood through two primary axes:

**Axis 1: Energetic Direction**
- **Activating** → Stimulates the sympathetic nervous system, raises energy, sharpens focus, generates heat, mobilises resources
- **Calming** → Stimulates the parasympathetic nervous system, lowers arousal, promotes recovery, cools, integrates

**Axis 2: Lineage**
- **Ancient** → Rooted in yogic, Buddhist, Taoist, or other traditional systems; developed over centuries of systematic practice
- **Modern** → Developed or significantly refined through 20th/21st century scientific investigation

This map is not exhaustive — it is a navigation tool. Your current state is the compass; your goal is the destination; this map shows the routes.

## Reading Your State: The Navigation Compass

Before choosing a practice, take 30 seconds to honestly assess where you are:

### State Assessment

**Are you exhausted or depleted?**
→ You need restoration, not activation
→ NSDR, Yoga Nidra, 4-7-8, Body Scan, Bhramari

**Are you anxious or stressed?**
→ You need parasympathetic activation, CO2 normalisation
→ Physiological Sigh (first), Box Breathing, 4-7-8, Nadi Shodhana, Coherence Breathing, Buteyko Reduced Breathing

**Are you sluggish, foggy, or unmotivated?**
→ You need sympathetic activation, CO2 reduction, oxygenation
→ Kapalabhati, Bhastrika, Wim Hof Method, Surya Bhedana

**Are you already balanced and want to go deeper?**
→ You need refinement, not correction
→ Nadi Shodhana, Anapanasati, Trataka, Yoga Nidra, Metta, Box Breathing at higher counts

**Are you in acute stress (spike, not chronic)?**
→ You need the fastest available reset
→ Physiological Sigh (1-3 cycles), then Box Breathing or coherence breathing

**Do you want to build long-term capacity?**
→ You need foundational practices done consistently
→ Buteyko Reduced Breathing (CO2 tolerance), Nadi Shodhana (nervous system balance), Anapanasati (attention training), Coherence Breathing (HRV building)

## Morning Practices

The morning is characterised by: high cortisol (the cortisol awakening response peaks 20-30 minutes after waking), relatively low adenosine (sleep pressure has been cleared), and an opportunity to set the neurological tone for the day.

### Ideal Morning Stack

**On waking (before anything else) — 5 minutes**:
Buteyko nasal breathing: simply ensure you are breathing through the nose from the moment of waking. This re-establishes the nasal habit after 8 hours of potentially compromised breathing.

**10-20 minutes after waking — 5-10 minutes**:
Wim Hof Method (if high activation desired) OR Kapalabhati (3 rounds, 30-50 repetitions) for mental clarity and abdominal massage.

**Before breakfast — 10-20 minutes**:
Nadi Shodhana (6-12 cycles) to balance the nervous system and establish meditative quality for the day, OR Mindfulness of breath if a calmer start is preferred.

**Alternative morning approach** (for those who prefer gradual activation):
- Slow nasal breathing in bed for 3 minutes before rising
- 10-minute body scan to arrive in the body
- 10 minutes of mindfulness of breath to establish the quality of attention
- Gentle Nadi Shodhana before beginning the day

### Morning Timing Considerations from Ayurveda

Ayurveda — the Indian system of medicine — divides the day into three-hour periods governed by different *doshas* (constitutional energies):

- **Vata** (air, ether): 2-6 AM and 2-6 PM — qualities of lightness, movement, creativity. Best for energising practices, creative work.
- **Kapha** (earth, water): 6-10 AM and 6-10 PM — qualities of heaviness, stability, groundedness. Best for activating practices that counter sluggishness; early morning kapha time is when most people feel slow — Kapalabhati or Wim Hof are ideal here.
- **Pitta** (fire, water): 10 AM-2 PM and 10 PM-2 AM — qualities of intensity, metabolism, digestion. The peak cognitive performance window; also the deepest sleep window at night.

Engaging in activating breathwork during the morning kapha period (6-10 AM) works with the body's natural tendency and produces excellent results. Activating practices during the pitta period (10 AM-2 PM) may be overdoing it — the body is already running hot.

## Evening Practices

The evening is characterised by: decreasing cortisol, rising melatonin (in darkness), rising adenosine (sleep pressure building), and the need for physiological and psychological downregulation.

### Ideal Evening Stack

**Post-work, pre-dinner — 10 minutes**:
Physiological sighs (2-3 cycles) to reset from the workday, followed by 5 minutes of coherence breathing (5-6 BPM) to restore HRV after daily stress accumulation.

**After dinner, before sleep preparation — 15-20 minutes**:
Yoga Nidra or body scan, OR Bhramari (10 minutes — the nitric oxide production is particularly beneficial before sleep).

**In bed before sleep — 5-10 minutes**:
4-7-8 breathing (3-6 cycles), or simply slow nasal breathing at 5-6 BPM (coherence breathing) while letting the body settle toward sleep.

### What to Avoid in the Evening

- Wim Hof Method: the adrenaline release will delay sleep onset. Practice in the morning.
- Kapalabhati or Bhastrika: activating practices counteract the natural melatonin rise.
- Extended Kumbhaka (especially internal retention): can activate rather than calm.
- Blue light screens immediately before sleep: reduces melatonin — if possible, finish screen use 30-60 minutes before sleep and spend that time with breath practice instead.

## Pre-Performance: The 10-Minute Reset

For athletes, performers, executives, or anyone facing a high-stakes situation:

**10-5-1 Protocol**:
- **10 minutes before**: Box breathing (4-4-4-4), 6-8 cycles. Calibrate the nervous system to alert, steady state.
- **5 minutes before**: 2-3 minutes of coherence breathing (5-6 BPM). Hold the calibration.
- **1 minute before**: One physiological sigh. Complete the reset.

---

## Recovery: The Post-Exertion Protocol

After intense exercise, acute stress, or emotionally demanding experiences:

1. **Immediately after**: Natural breathing. Don't force anything. Let the body's own recovery mechanisms begin.
2. **5-10 minutes after**: Shift to nasal-only breathing, reducing the volume gradually as recovery progresses. This accelerates CO2 normalisation.
3. **15-20 minutes after**: If time allows, 20 minutes of NSDR or Yoga Nidra. Research shows that this dramatically accelerates both physiological and neurological recovery, consolidates skill learning, and restores dopamine levels.

## Seasonal Considerations

Ayurvedic and traditional Chinese medicine both adjust practice recommendations for the seasons — recognising that the body's relationship with energy, rest, and activity shifts with the cycles of light, temperature, and the natural world.

**Spring** (Kapha season — heavy, wet, building): Emphasise activating practices — Kapalabhati, Bhastrika, Wim Hof. The body is emerging from winter heaviness and benefits from energising, purifying practices.

**Summer** (Pitta season — hot, intense, productive): Balance is key. Cool practices — Chandra Bhedana (left nostril, cooling), Bhramari, Nadi Shodhana. Avoid adding heat to an already hot season. Cold water exposure becomes more valuable.

**Autumn** (Vata season — dry, light, variable): Grounding practices. Longer, slower, warming practices. Yoga Nidra, body scan, coherence breathing, Metta. The variable quality of autumn benefits from stability.

**Winter** (Kapha/Vata transition — cold, dark, inward): Inward and warming practices. Wim Hof (generates internal heat), deep meditation, Yoga Nidra, extended pranayama in the warmth. Winter is the natural time for deep inner work.

## Intentional Nervous System Navigation

The most important skill that a breathwork practice develops — beyond any specific technique — is the ability to navigate your own nervous system state deliberately.

Most people are passengers in their own autonomic nervous system, reacting to whatever state they happen to find themselves in. A developed practice turns you into the driver: someone who can read their current state accurately, choose the appropriate intervention, and shift state deliberately in the direction of what the moment requires.

This skill — **state awareness + intentional state transition** — is the foundation of emotional regulation, high performance, resilience under pressure, and the capacity for deep meditation. The techniques are vehicles. The ability to navigate is the destination.

As this skill matures, it becomes less and less about formal practice sessions and more about a continuous, fluid relationship with your own nervous system throughout the day. Breathing becomes not something you do for 20 minutes in the morning, but something you do — consciously, skilfully — all day long.

This is what the ancient traditions meant by *prana awareness*: not just formal practice, but a pervasive attunement to the subtle currents of energy and state that constitute the living moment.
